# Theme Stack — YouTube Demo Script (Investor/Customer-Facing)

---

**HOOK (0:00–0:30)**  
Hi — I want to show you something I built to solve the problem I actually had as a serious personal investor. It wasn’t about predicting the market better. It was about **not getting lost in it**.

I call it **Theme Stack**. Think of it as a second brain: it takes hundreds of documents and analyses every day and turns them into a structured view of what the market is really trading on, so I can stay oriented instead of reactive.

---

**WHY THIS EXISTS (0:30–1:15)**  
I treat investing as a second career and a real source of income, but I’m not at a fund — I only see what’s public. So the question for me wasn’t “how do I get an edge?” It was: **where can AI actually help?**

And here’s the thing: most analysts aren’t meaningfully better than a coin flip at forecasting. The real bottleneck is **information overload**. We drown in news, we forget what mattered, and we end up making emotional decisions. As a part-time investor I don’t have the hours to read everything, and my attention gets pulled in a hundred directions by headlines and KOLs.

So I didn’t build a prediction machine. I built what I think of as a **structure machine** — something that organizes how I process information so I can develop better intuition and make fewer dumb, reactive moves.

---

**WHAT IT DOES — IN MY ORDER OF THINKING (1:15–4:00)**  

**1. Ingest from the sources I actually trust**  
Theme Stack pulls from what I already read: Substack newsletters, equity research, earnings transcripts, company results. X and WeChat are on the roadmap. When I actually sat down and listed everything I follow, it was dozens of sources — and I was forgetting most of it. Now it’s one pipeline, and the system **extracts what I’d care about**, not just “documents.”

**2. Themes and sentiment — the way I actually think**  
I’ve always processed the market by **themes and narratives**, not by document. So the system tags sentiment and surfaces **new concepts** before they go mainstream — things like HBM memory, CPO — so I can decide whether to learn them early instead of chasing late. It also broadens my map. I’ve discovered themes like “pharma in space,” “AI-insulated in-person experiences,” “K-pop’s economic model” — areas I wouldn’t have had time to explore without this. I’m not betting on everything; I’m just staying open and informed.

**3. Re-orient, don’t chase the headline**  
Every day I get a view of **what’s prevailing** across all that input. So for example, “Brent oil” might be dominating because of geopolitics, but underneath the noise you can see that “software” and AI disruption are **gaining just as much attention** — which tells me the market isn’t only trading the war. I also see themes on a **quantitative scale**: what’s emerging, what’s turning bullish or negative, what’s the most positive and most negative right now. So even on busy days, the dashboard tells me what’s changed and I don’t have to re-read everything.

**4. Fact vs opinion — where I look for edge**  
I segment sentiment into **fact vs opinion**. Facts are things like DAU, reported results, concrete data. Opinions are price targets, scenarios, “what could happen.” And I’ve found that **opportunities often show up when facts and opinions diverge.** I even ran a check on whether analyst price targets actually help — and a lot of the time they don’t beat the market; they tend to be too negative in downturns and too positive in upturns. So the tool is built to highlight **what’s grounded versus what’s narrative.**

**5. Themes I follow — baskets, data, and “what’s new”**  
For themes I care about, I go deeper. I use AI to suggest a **basket of companies** tied to the theme, and I hook up **market data** — prices vs SPX, fundamentals, technicals. I use EODHD for reliability and international coverage, which matters for my book. Then I don’t have to read every report. **Daily summaries** tell me what the market has agreed on for this theme, what’s changed, what it’s waiting for, and what it’s worrying about. So even if I skip a day, I don’t lose the thread.

**6. Track what matters in natural language**  
The world changes, and **tracking how narratives change** matters more to me than reading any single report. I’d always wished there was a way to get alerts when X or Y happened — so I built it. I write in plain language what I want to track. For Baidu, for instance, I care about “share of revenue from AI,” “profit margin,” “Apollo’s overseas expansion,” because I’m watching whether the story is shifting from search decline to AI and robotaxi. The system **refreshes those questions** from the docs I’ve ingested and from market and fundamental data, so I’m not re-searching every time I open the app. This part is still evolving, but it’s core to how I want to work.

**7. Original sources when I need them**  
The narrative extractions are usually accurate and concise. When I want more detail, I jump to the **original document**. The pipeline handles long PDFs and keeps context — I went through a few models and landed on one that fits that job. The goal is **narratives first, drill-down when it matters.**

---

**MY MENTAL MODEL — WHAT THE TOOL ENABLES (4:00–4:30)**  
The system is built to support how I actually decide. I discover themes and build intuition about what the market is trading. When I have high conviction and low valuation, I buy dips. When I have high conviction and OK valuation, I might wait, range-trade, or sell puts on dips. When conviction is high but valuation is rich, I take profits.

AI doesn’t make the call. It **organizes and executes** that mental model so I can run it consistently even when I’m not full-time on the market.

---

**PRODUCT PHILOSOPHY — WHY IT’S DESIGNED THIS WAY (4:30–5:00)**  
I’m not interested in a black-box money printer. I actually like reading, understanding how the world works, and finding companies I believe in. So the product is built to **half-automate the process, not replace the thinking.**

And one more thing: reading from a single source is suboptimal. When Nike says “consumer spending is weak,” that’s not just a Nike story — it matters for the whole consumer sector. So the design is **theme- and narrative-centric, cross-source**. We aggregate by what the market is talking about, not by document. That’s the product thinking: **structure that fits how an investor’s brain works**, not how a filing system works.

---

**WRAP (5:00–5:20)**  
Theme Stack runs on my MacBook, with docs and extracted themes stored in the cloud so my machine isn’t the bottleneck. Cost-wise, data and APIs run in the tens of dollars per month at my usage; the heavier part is market data for the coverage I need.

So that’s my name card: **using AI to turn information overload into structure**, so I can stay calmer in distress and more skeptical when a trade gets crowded.
